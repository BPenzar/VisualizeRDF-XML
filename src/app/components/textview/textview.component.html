Iranian Jewellery and small finds in religious context

By Natascha Bagherpour Kashani

Keywords; Iran, Parthian, Sasanian, finger-rings, beads.

واژگان کلیدی: ایران، اشکانیان، ساسانیان، انگشترها، مهره ها

The items discovered in the Cale Gar mines at Vesnave have added a fresh perspective to the investigation of pre-lslamic religion, as emphasized by

Overlaet, J. Rose and P. Kreyenbroek.1 The finds from Vesnave make an important contribution to our knowledge, not only for researching pre-lslamic religion, but also for visualising the physical artefacts of ancient Iran.

The importance of the study of jewellery finds from Vesnave

In Vesnave, two mines were excavated by a team of German and Iranian archaeologists in several campaigns from 2001 to 2004.2 They discovered, mainly in Cale Gar 1, a great number of artefacts, among them jewellery - including many beads ,finger-rings, ear-rings and pendants - and fragments of metal sheets , as well as much else. It is remarkable that these objects were found in such a con- text at Vesnave, because in Iran and the wider Near East, jewellery has been unearthed predominantly in graves. To excavate such an amount of artefacts in the Cale Gar caves has been, apart from their importance for the investigation of the pre-lslamic religion, a milestone for research into small finds in Iran and the Near East in general.

The study of these objects is of great interest for the understanding of ritual practices at Vesnave, since establishing their chronology and provenance may help broaden knowledge of personal ornaments in ancient Iran.

The finds

For a description of mine Gale Gar 1 , the reader is referred to the article by Thomas Stollner in this volume. A large amount of artefacts were discovered there, while Cale Gar 2 yielded just a few finds from a humid corner. Ceramics, whether complete vessels or fragments, were found in mine 1; further fragments of textiles , animal bones , wood and wooden vessels , as well as other organic material like nuts and baked remains , were also found there.

Among the finds from Cale Gar 1 and 2, small finds - jewellery artefacts primarily - represent a significant group. First and foremost, beads and pendants are a major group, made by different techniques and from different materials, but mainly glass and stone. Furthermore, coins, metal finger and ear-rings and a glass finger-ring were discovered, as well as figurative objects, inlays, settings, chain links, a few ornaments for clothes, bells and metal sheets (plain or with figurative or plant depiction

The date of the Sacred Cave 1

So far, it has proven possible to date mine 1 at Vesnave roughly by C-analysis and by coin finds. From the bottom of the cultic context, charcoal was collected and analysed. The beginning of cultic activity can thus be assigned to 900-781 BC, while the use of the site seems to have ended in the early Islamic period, based on the final coin find (Fig. I).10 This coin is a silver derham of the Umayyad period. Due to its state of preservation, it is not possible to read the date, but limits can be set between 661 and 750 AD. Other finds seem to confirm these chronological limits for the sacrificial cave (see below).

Although charcoal and coins indicate the beginning and the rough end of the use of the cave as a holy place, they can obviously not demonstrate the kind of use, the manner of deposition and the changes during the c. 1500 years of its existence for that purpose. Examining jewellery found in Cale Gar 1 and 2 will answer some of these questions. Investigations may be able to specify the dates of the deposits and give insights into the procedure of deposition.

Following is a general introduction to the beads and finger-rings from Vesnave, followed by the presentation of selected find groups, such as gold-in-glass beads, etched carnelian beads and finger-rings with bird ornaments, spirals and one find with a conical bezel.

Beads, pendants and spacers

Beads - quantified simply by their number - account for the largest part of small finds at Vesnave. They consist of many different materials, but predominantly glass, and were fabricated using different

techniques. The excavators found beads of monochrome opaque or translucent glass, and their colours were mainly yellow, green, dark/black, brown and different shades of blue. Few beads were turquoise, lilac, orange or white. An analysis of the material has not yet been carried out, therefore concerning the opaque beads, it is not clear whether they were originally manufactured of frit or glass; but it seems that they mainly consist of glass.12

Some beads are multi-coloured, others consist of two different coloured layers. So-called mosaic beads, different kinds of eye beads and so-called gold-in-glass beads stand out from the assemblage.13 Their manufacturing processes are of great interest; that used for the gold-in-glass beads will be explained below. Besides colours, the Vesnave beads have different shapes; they vary from simple spherical beads to disc, cylindrical, double-conical, rectangular, rhombic, hexagonal shapes, corner-less cube and so-called melon-shaped beads (No. 6448, Fig. 2).

Besides glass ones, stone beads make up a large proportion of the finds. These are mainly carnelian beads of shapes similar to those named above; but other materials used include garnet, jet, chalcedony, rock crystal, hematite, plus a few of turquoise and lapis lazuli of poor quality. More precious beads were made of silver, gold and amber, while some other beads were made of bone, cowry and other shells.

A significant category discovered solely in mine 1 are the so-called etched carnelian beads. These artefacts are of simple shapes and ornamented by white zig-zag lines, waves, dots or a “plant-like” pattern (Fig. 3,a-c). As mentioned above. Beck, “the founding father of bead studies”, was the first to establish a chronology of the manufacture of etched carnelian beads. He distinguished three phases: the early period. A, which ended ca. 2000 BC; the middle period, B, from 300 BC to AD 200; and the late period, C, AD 600 to 1000. This chronological framework is still consulted frequently as an important source for studies of etched carnelian beads. However, hiatuses in the dating are obvious. Later works by E. Mackay, M. Dikshit, E. During Caspers, J. Reade, A. de Waele and E. Haerinck expanded Beck's studies, but did not fill the gaps completely. Although no recent study of etched beads has succeeded in presenting an adapted chronology for the entire Near East, etched carnelian beads are discussed more often than other kinds of stone beads, because, unlike plain carnelian beads, their ornamentation tends to be chronologically significant.

There can be little room for doubt that carnelian beads were etched by a similar process in pre-Islamic times. Besides technical capability, learning from experience is essential to etch stones: Not every carnelian is equally fireproof: some stones turn pink, white or even opaque when heated too much or too long.22

Dating the etched carnelian beads

At Vesnave, seventeen ornamented carnelian beads were found. They vary from fragmented to the complete, and from orange to red in colour. They correspond to Beck’s Type I with a white pattern on bare stone.23 By comparing parallel finds from Hassani Mahale, Rudbar --place-- and Djuben --place--, all sites in the northern Iranian province of Gilan --place--, it would appear that the Cale Gar beads with simple patterns of dots or lines should be dated to the 1st mill. BC; but their rough shape, careless decoration and the colour of the stone indicate a later dating, i.e. at least the end of the 1st mill. BC or the 1st centuries AD.24 Other beads with zig-zags, dots and crosswise decorations seem to belong to the centuries after Christ, particularly the Sasanian period, i.e. from the 3rd to the 7th century.25 Analogies are known from the region of Germi in northern Iran and from Ed-Dur in Arabia.26

Gold-in-glass beads

As impressive as the etched beads are the so-called gold-in-glass beads, which have been discovered in Cale Gar 1. They have not been found in large numbers, but they are beautifully produced pieces for which a special technique was employed: a gold foil is applied on a glass core and then enclosed in translucent, colourless glass. This manufacturing technique gave them a fine appearance, and analysing it helps to date them. These beads were abundant from Antiquity to Medieval times, and have been discovered in Asia, as well as in Europe, including Great Britain. On the island of Rhodes, remains of unfinished gold-in-glass beads were found and dated to the late 3rd cent. BC.

G. Boon and Spaer suggest Egypt as the region of origin for the gold-in-glass method of manufacture, while D. Barag argues that it emanated from Phoenician roots and other scholars prefer India. As an early instance of gold-in-glass manufacturing, the so-called Canosa Group of the 1st half of the 3rd cent. BC has been considered by several scholars. This refers to two well preserved glass bowls with decorated golden inlays, found alongside eight glasses, in a grave in southern Italy, having probably been produced in a Ptolemaic workshop. According to D. Harden and A. Oliver, no gold-in-glass beads have been found of an earlier date than the Canosa Group. This fact, together with the remains of the glass workshop at Rhodes that date to the late 3rd century BC, implies that gold-in-glass beads were not produced before the 3rd cent. BC, in particular the late 3rd century. As mentioned above, such beads continued to be produced into medieval times, though evidence for continuity of manufacture in the Islamic Near East is scarce.

The gold-in-glass technique

Special skill and experience were needed to produce gold-in-glass beads. The craftsman had to put a flimsy gold sheet on a glass core and cover this combination with translucent glass. The two glass layers should then respond equally to heat, i.e. they should have the same coefficient of expansion; otherwise the glass would break during the process. Another challenge was not to harm the gold foil that could be as thin as 0.0001 mm, and so was very easily damaged.35 According to Spaer, earlier beads were finished individually, and show larger string-holes and a thicker exterior glass layer; these were of better quality. In later times beads were treated with tools and probably moulds.36 Grooves in the glass point to fabrication of the beads by drawing glass tubes: “A drawn tube was, after cooling, covered with a very thin layer of metal foil, probably attached with the aid of an adhesive. Another, slightly larger, pre-manufactured tube was then slipped over the first.”. This gold-in-glass tube was then divided by a tool into single beads, or into beads with two or more segments. Spaer writes furthermore: “(...) There is nonetheless a clear indication that the beads finished individually by hot-working are Hellenistic and probably, at least in part, also early Roman. Beads finished by the use of segmenting molds are mainly Roman or later ”38
